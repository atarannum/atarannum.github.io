Bengali Language Movement

The Bengali language movement in East Bengal (now Bangladesh) aimed to secure Bengali in governance, education, media, currency, & writing.

Afsana Tarannum

March 10, 2024

01 / 05
1

University of Dhaka

On February 21st, 1952, at nine in the morning, students started assembling on the premises of the University of Dhaka. Despite the legal restriction imposed by Section 144, which prohibited public gatherings, their determination to uphold their cause overshadowed any apprehensions. Their gathering was not merely an act of disobedience but a testament to their unwavering commitment to the preservation and recognition of the Bengali language.

2

Dhaka University Gate

By eleven fifteen, students assembled at the university gate and made an effort to breach the police barricade. In response, the police discharged tear gas shells in the direction of the gate as a warning to the students.

3

Dhaka Medical College

A group of students sought refuge in the Dhaka Medical College, while another group moved towards the university premises, which were surrounded by police. The vice-chancellor requested the police to halt their firing and directed the students to evacuate the premises. However, as the students attempted to comply and leave the area, the police arrested several of them for violating Section 144.

Infuriated by the arrests, the students gathered near the then East Bengal Legislative Assembly and demanded the legislators to voice their grievances in the assembly. As some students attempted to enter the building, the police opened fire, resulting in the deaths of several students.

4

Curzon Hall

On February 22, 1952, disorder swept across the province as significant processions disregarded Section 144 and criticized the actions of the police. Over 30,000 individuals gathered at Curzon Hall in Dhaka. Amid ongoing protests, police actions resulted in the deaths of four additional individuals. In response, officers and employees from various organizations, including colleges, banks, and the radio station, chose to boycott their offices and join the procession.

5

Monument of Martyrs

On February 23, students of Dhaka Medical College worked through the night to construct a Shaheed Smritistombho, or Monument of Martyrs, completed by dawn on February 24. Unfortunately, it was destroyed by police on February 26. Despite setbacks, the Language Movement continued, leading to Bengali gaining official status alongside Urdu in Pakistan in 1956. Delayed by martial law, the monument was eventually rebuilt in 1963 but demolished during the Bangladesh Liberation War in 1971. After independence, it was reconstructed and expanded in 1983.
